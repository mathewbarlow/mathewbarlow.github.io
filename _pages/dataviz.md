I find that a good visual graphic is very useful for public outreach, communication to specialist audiences, teaching, and my own understanding. The availability of both free and open-source visualization packages as well as free online access to a wide range of Earth Science data offer many options for anyone with a computer and internet access to make graphics that previoiusly took a dedicated team of specialists to create. Exciting times!  

One of my main goals with data visualization is to help effectively convey scientific information to the public and decision-makers. However, I think it is also import to help people re-engage with the excitement, curiosity, and sense of wonder that comes from science and understanding how the natural world works.

I primarily work with Python, Blender, ParaView, and standard desktop software to make visualizations. I also have experimented a bit with 3D printing.

For some examples of my visualization images, please see my [image repository.](https://github.com/mathewbarlow/data_visualization)

For some examples of visualization animations, please see my [animation repository](https://github.com/mathewbarlow/animations) and my [gallery of atmospheric dynamics animations](https://storm.uml.edu/~metweb/newBlog/wordpress/2020/05/14/gallery-of-atmospheric-dynamics-animations/).

As personal computers become ever more powerful, it is now possible to run interactive fluid dynamics simulations through a web browser. I find  [VisualPDE](https://visualpde.com) to be an amazing tool for this, and have made several examples which may be viewed (with links to run them) in my [VisualPDE repository](https://github.com/mathewbarlow/VisualPDE-programs).
