Society benefits when decisions are supported by the best available science. Therefore, I think it is important for scientists, especially those supported by public funds, to help communicate scientific results in engagement with the public and decision makers. This is especially important for topics of critical societal importance such as human-caused climate change but I think it is also important for all aspects of science, including basic research. Many of the most [societally-useful scientific results have come as the result of basic research originally aimed at a completely different topic](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-03474-x), and it is important to both the public and to the practice (and support) of science that the public and decision-makers understand the benefits of both applied and basic research. I'm happy to note that my department's PhD program has a required core course in communication.

From a science communciation perspective, it is important to keep in mind that communication on politically-controversial topics like climate change is not simply a matter of providing facts to the uninformed (the [information deficit model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_deficit_model)) but also needs to involve contextualization and dialogue. It is also important to note that doubt about the human causes of climate change is the intentional product of well-funded efforts, as extensively documented by, for instance, Oreskes and Conway in ["Merchants of Doubt"](https://www.merchantsofdoubt.org/home/authors/). Fortunately, however, a majority of people in the US [do understand the climate change is occuring and is mostly caused by human activities](https://climatecommunication.yale.edu/visualizations-data/ycom-us/).

My science communciation work has included writing [general audience pieces for The Conversation](https://theconversation.com/profiles/mathew-barlow-1256924/articles), providing information to numerous media outlets, participating in [AGU's Voices for Science](https://www.agu.org/honors/voices-for-science), and contributing to climate assessments at the [local](https://greenribboncommission.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/06/GBRAG_report_05312022@1915.pdf), state, national, and [international](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-8/) levels.
